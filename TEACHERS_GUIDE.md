# Peppered Moth Evolution Simulator — Teacher's Guide

## What This Does
Students watch a population of peppered moths evolve in real-time based on predation pressure. Moths that contrast with tree bark get eaten; moths that blend in survive and reproduce.

## Learning Objectives
- **Natural Selection**: Understand how environmental pressures shape populations
- **Industrial Melanism**: Historical case study of evolution in action (UK 1800s-2000s)
- **Adaptation**: Track how quickly populations shift when conditions change

## Quick Start (2 minutes)
1. Download `index.html` to any computer
2. Open it in any web browser (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge)
3. Click "Start Simulation"
4. No installs, no accounts, no internet required after download

## Suggested Lesson Plan

### Part 1: The Baseline (10 min)
1. Set **Tree Color** to "Clean (Light)"
2. Set **Initial Light Moth %** to 50%
3. Click **Start Simulation**
4. **Observe**: Light moths quickly dominate
5. **Discussion**: Why did this happen?

### Part 2: Industrial Revolution (10 min)
1. Pause the simulation
2. Change **Tree Color** to "Industrial (Dark)" — represents soot-covered trees
3. Resume simulation
4. **Observe**: Dark moths now have advantage
5. **Discuss**: How does this mirror history? (UK 1850-1950)

### Part 3: Modern Clean Air Laws (10 min)
1. Pause again
2. Switch back to "Clean (Light)" — represents modern pollution controls
3. Resume
4. **Observe**: Population shifts back
5. **Connect**: Real peppered moths in UK did exactly this (1970s-2000s)

### Part 4: Mixed Forest Challenge (15 min)
1. Reset simulation
2. Try "Mixed Forest" mode (half light trees, half dark)
3. **Question**: Which moth type wins? (Answer: Both coexist!)
4. **Extension**: Discuss habitat diversity → species diversity

## Discussion Questions

### Beginner
- What color moth is safest on light trees?
- How many generations did it take to see a clear shift?
- What would happen if we started with 90% dark moths on light trees?

### Intermediate
- Why doesn't one type go completely extinct? (Mutation + reproduction randomness)
- How is this different from "moths choosing" to change color? (Selection, not will)
- What if predation rate is 80% vs 10%? (Faster/slower evolution)

### Advanced
- In mixed forest mode, why do both types persist?
- How does population size affect evolution speed? (Try 50 vs 300 moths)
- What's the role of mutation rate in this model?

## Extension Activities

### Data Collection
Have students record:
- Generation when light moths hit 80%
- Generation when dark moths hit 80%
- Compare with different predation rates

### Graph Interpretation
The population chart shows:
- 🟡 Light moths
- ⚫ Dark moths
- 🔵 Total population

Ask: "When does total population drop? Why?" (High predation)

### Historical Research
After simulation, have students research:
- Real peppered moth (Biston betularia)
- UK Industrial Revolution impact
- Modern recovery after Clean Air Acts

## Comparison to Real Data
Real peppered moth evolution (UK):
- 1800s: ~95% light moths
- 1950s: ~90% dark moths (peak industrialization)
- 2000s: ~90% light moths (post-Clean Air Act)

Your simulation should produce similar timeframes (~50-100 generations for major shifts).

## Troubleshooting
- **Simulation too slow?** Increase speed to "Fast" or "Instant"
- **Can't see moths?** Zoom in with browser (Ctrl/Cmd +)
- **Chart not updating?** Needs at least 2 generations of data
